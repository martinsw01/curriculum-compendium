    Noetherian and Irreducible Spaces

    Definition 3.7

    Noetherian and Irreducible Spaces

    Let \(X\) be a topological space.

    \(X\) is called noetherian if closed subsets satisfies d.c.c.

    Thus, the open subsets satisfies the a.c.c.. For \(X=\Spec R\), this induces ascending chains of ideals.

    \(X\) is called irreducible if \(X\neq \emptyset\), and cannot be written as the union of two closed proper subsets. This implies connectedness.

    Example 3.8
    1. \(\R^n\) and \(\C^n\) are not Noetherian.
    2. \(|X| < \infty \implies X\) is Noetherian.
    3. \(|X| = 1 \implies X\) is irreducible.
    4. \(|K|=\infty \implies K^1\) the affine line is irreducible.
    Theorem 3.9

    Noether property of the Zariski topology

    Let \(X\subseteq K^n\) be a set of points equipped with the Zariski-topology.

    \[\implies X \text{ is Noetherian}\]

    Let \(R\) be a Noetherian ring and \(X\subseteq \Spec R\) equipped with the Zariski-topology.

    \[\implies X \text{ is Noetherian}\]
    Proof

    Assume without loss of generality that \(X=K^n\).

    Let \(\{X_i\}\) be a descending sequence of closed sets in \(X\).

    Then, \(\I(X_i)\) is an ascending sequence of ideals in \(K[\underline x]\), which is Noehterian. It must therefore stabilize. Hence, the chain \(\{X_i\}=\{\V(\I(X_i))\}\) must stabilise.

    Assume without loss of generality that \(X=\Spec R\).

    Let \(\{X_i\}\) be a descending sequence of closed sets in \(X\).

    Then, \(\I(X_i)\) is an ascending sequence of ideals in \(R\), which is Noehterian. It must therefore stabilize. Hence, the chain \(\{X_i\}=\{\V(\I(X_i))\}\) must stabilise.

    Theorem 3.10

    Irreducible subsets

    Let \(I = \I(X)\).

    \[X \subseteq K^n \text{ is irreducible } \iff \I(X) \text{ is a prime ideal}\]
    \[X \subseteq \Spec R \text{ is irreducible } \iff \I(X) \text{ is a prime ideal}\]
    Proof

    Let \(\V:=\V_K\) and \(\I:=\I_{K[\underline x]}\) as defined in respectively D.1.6 and D.1.15.

    First of all, \(X\) being irreducible implies \(X \neq \emptyset\) and \(\I(X) \properideal K[\underline x]\).

    \(\begin{align*} \text{Let } &f_1, f_2 \in K[\underline x] \text{ s.t.} \\ & f_1 f_2 \in I \end{align*}\)

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies X \subseteq \V(f_1 f_2) = \V(f_1) \cup \V(f_2) \\ &\implies \left(X \cap \V(f_1)\right) \cup \left(X \cap \V(f_2)\right) \\ &\overset{X \text{ irreducible}}{\implies} X = X \cap \V(f_1), & i \in \{1, 2\} \\ &\implies f_i \in I \\ &\implies I \text{ is prime} & & \square \end{align*} \]

    As \(I\) is a prime ideal, \(X\neq \emptyset\).

    Assume \(X = X_1 \cap X_2\) is a union of two closed subsets in X.

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies X_i = X \cap \V(I_i) & I_i \ideal K[\underline x] \\ &\implies X \subseteq \V(I_1) \cup \V(I_2) \eqhref{../affine_varieties/#p31}{P.3.1} \V(I_1 \cap I_2) \\ &\implies I_1 \cap I_2 \subseteq \I(X) = I \\ &\implies \exists\ i \in\{1, 2\} : I_i \subseteq I & \text{as } I \text{ is prime} \\ &\implies X \subseteq \V(I_i) \\ &\implies X = X \cap \V(I_i) = X_i && \square \end{align*} \]

    Let \(\V:=\V_{\Spec R}\) and \(\I:=\I_R\) as defined in D.3.5.

    First of all, \(X\) being irreducible implies \(X \neq \emptyset\) and \(\I(X) \properideal R\).

    \(\begin{align*} \text{Let } &a_1, a_2 \in R \text{ s.t.} \\ & a_1 a_2 \in I \end{align*}\)

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies X \subseteq \V(a_1 a_2) = \V(a_1) \cup \V(a_2) \\ &\implies \left(X \cap \V(a_1)\right) \cup \left(X \cap \V(a_2)\right) \\ &\overset{X \text{ irreducible}}{\implies} X = X \cap \V(a_1), & i \in \{1, 2\} \\ &\implies a_i \in I \\ &\implies I \text{ is prime} & & \square \end{align*} \]

    As \(I\) is a prime ideal, \(X\neq \emptyset\).

    Assume \(X = X_1 \cap X_2\) is a union of two closed subsets in X.

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies X_i = X \cap \V(I_i) & I_i \ideal R \\ &\implies X \subseteq \V(I_1) \cup \V(I_2) \eqhref{../affine_varieties/#p31}{P.3.1} \V(I_1 \cap I_2) \\ &\implies I_1 \cap I_2 \subseteq \I(X) = I \\ &\implies \exists\ i \in\{1, 2\} : I_i \subseteq I & \text{as } I \text{ is prime} \\ &\implies X \subseteq \V(I_i) \\ &\implies X = X \cap \V(I_i) = X_i && \square \end{align*} \]
    Theorem 3.11

    Decomposition into irreducibles

    Let \(X\) be a Noetherian topological space.

    There are closed irreducible subsets \(Z_1, ..., Z_n \subseteq X\) with \(n \in \N_0\) and \(Z_i \subsetneq Z_j\ \forall\ i\neq j\) s.t.

    \[X = \bigcup_{i=1}^n Z_i\]

    If the closed irreducible \(Z_1, ..., Z_n \subseteq X\) satisfies a), then every irreducible subset \(Z\subseteq X\) is contained in a \(Z_i\).

    If the closed irreducible \(Z_1, ..., Z_n \subseteq X\) satisfies a), then they are the maximal irreducible subsets of \(X\).

    Moreover, they are uniquelly determined up to order.

    Proof

    As \(X\) is Noetherian, every nonempty closed subset of \(X\) contains a minimal closed subset (AoC).

    Assume \(X\) has a closed subset \(Y \subseteq Y\) that is not a finite union of closed irreducible subsets. Without loss of generality, we may assume \(Y\) to be minimal.

    By assumption, \(Y = Y_1 \cap Y_2\) with \(Y_1, Y_2 \subsetneq Y\) closed subsets. However, as \(Y\) is minimal, \(Y_i\) must be a finite union of closed irreducible subsets. Thus, so is \(Y\), which is a contradiction.

    Thus, \(X=\bigcup_{i=1}^n Y_i\) is a finite union of closed irreducible subsets. Assuming the \(Y_i\)s to be distinct, and removeing the \(Y_i\) s.t. \(Y_i \subseteq Y_j\), we get a decomposition described in the theorem.

    Let \(Z \subseteq X\) be an irreducible subset.

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies Z = \bigcup_{i=1}^n (Z \cap Z_i) \\ &\overset{Z \text{ irred.}}{\implies} \exists\ i \in \{1, ..., n\} : Z \subseteq Z_i \\ \end{align*} \]

    Let \(Z\subseteq X\) be a maximal irreducible subset.

    \(\begin{align*} \overset{b)}{\implies} Z \subseteq Z_i \overset{\text{maximal}}{\implies} Z=Z_i \end{align*}\)

    Thus, the maximal irreducible subsets are among the \(Z_i\)s. It remains to be shown that they are the only ones:

    Assume there exists a \(Z_i \subseteq Z\). As \(Z\) is irreducible, by b), \(\exists\ j\) s.t. \(Z \subseteq Z_j\). Then, from a), we get \(Z_i = Z = Z_j.\tab\square\)

    Definnition 3.12

    Irreducible components

    The \(Z_i\) in T.3.11 are called irreducible components.

    Example 3.13

    Let \(f\in K[\underline x]\backslash K\).

    \[ \begin{align*} &\implies f = \prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{e_i}, & p_i \nsim p_j \iff i \neq j \text{ irreducible polynomials} \\ &\implies \V_{K^n}(f) = \bigcup_{i=1}^r \V_{K^n}(p_i) \end{align*} \]
    Corollary 3.14

    Minimal prime ideals

    Let \(R\) be a Noetherian ring.

    \[R \text{ has finitely many prime ideals } P_1, ..., P_n \ideal R.\]
    \[\text{Every prime ideal contains a minimal prime ideal.}\]
    \[\nil(R) = \bigcap_{i=1}^n P_i = \sqrt{\{0\}}\]

    Let \(I \ideal R\) be an ideal.

    \[\V_{\Spec R}(I) \text{ contains finitely many minimal elements } Q_1, ..., Q_n.\]
    \[\sqrt I = \bigcap_{i=1}^n Q_i\]
    Proof

    By P.3.6e) and T.3.10b) (minimal) prime ideals in \(R\) cooresponds to (maximal) closed irreducible subsets of \(\Spec R\).

    Then, by T.3.11c) there exists only finitely many minimal prime ideals. \(\tab\square\)

    Follows from T.3.11b) \(\tab\square\)

    From C.1.12, it follows that

    \[\nil(R) = \bigcap_{P\in\Spec R}\!\!\!\!\! P\ \ \overset{a)}{=} \bigcap_{i=1}^n P_i \tab\square\]

    Apply a) + c) to \(R/I \tab \square\)

    \[R \text{ is Noetherian } \iff R \text{ is Noetherian and } \Spec R = \Specmax R\]

    See original statement.

    By C.3.14, we have that
